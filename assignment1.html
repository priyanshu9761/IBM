International Business Machines Corporation (IBM) is an American multinational technology corporation headquartered in Armonk, New York and present in over 175 countries. IBM is the largest industrial research organization in the world, with 19 research facilities across a dozen countries, having held the record for most annual U.S. patents generated by a business for 29 consecutive years from 1993 to 2021.[1]

IBM produces and sells computer hardware, middleware and software, and provides hosting and consulting services in areas ranging from mainframe computers to nanotechnology. IBM is also a major research organization, holding the record for most annual U.S. patents generated by a business for 28 consecutive years (1993–2020).[2]

Inventions by IBM include the automated teller machine (ATM), the floppy disk, the hard disk drive, the magnetic stripe card, the relational database, the SQL programming language, the UPC barcode, and dynamic random-access memory (DRAM). The IBM mainframe, exemplified by the System/360, was the dominant computing platform during the 1960s and 1970s.[3]

History

IBM originated from the bringing together of several companies that worked to automate routine business transactions, including the first companies to build time clocks and punch card machines. IBM was incorporated in the state of New York on June 16, 1911 as the Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company (CTR) through a merger of the Tabulating Machine Company, the International Time Recording Company, and the Computing Scale Company.[4]

CTR was renamed "International Business Machines" in 1924, a name that captured the company's new scope of operations. The company began as a manufacturer of punch card tabulating machines and related products for statistical work. Over the decades, IBM has continually shifted its business mix by exiting commoditizing markets and entering higher-value markets.[5]

Early years (1911–1940s)

The company's first major breakthrough came with the development of the IBM 701 in 1952, IBM's first commercial scientific computer. This marked the beginning of IBM's transformation from a tabulating machine company to a computer company. The success of the 701 led to the development of the IBM 650, which became one of the most popular early computers.[6]

Products and services

IBM's current portfolio includes cloud computing, artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and various enterprise software and hardware solutions. The company has made significant investments in hybrid cloud and AI technologies, particularly through acquisitions like Red Hat in 2019 for $34 billion.[7]

Cloud and Cognitive Software

IBM's software portfolio includes IBM Cloud, Watson AI platform, and various middleware solutions. The Watson platform provides AI services for businesses across different industries, from healthcare to finance.[8]

Global Business Services

IBM Consulting (formerly IBM Global Business Services) provides consulting, application management, and business process outsourcing services. It is one of the world's largest consulting organizations with approximately 160,000 employees globally.[9]

Research and development

IBM operates 19 research facilities worldwide and has been granted more patents than any other U.S.-based technology company. IBM researchers have been awarded five Nobel Prizes, six Turing Awards, and numerous other honors. Notable IBM inventions include Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM), the relational database, RISC architecture, and scanning tunneling microscope.[10]

Corporate affairs

IBM is headquartered in Armonk, New York, and trades on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol IBM. The company is a component of the Dow Jones Industrial Average and the S&P 500. As of 2023, IBM employs approximately 288,300 people worldwide and operates in over 175 countries.[11]
